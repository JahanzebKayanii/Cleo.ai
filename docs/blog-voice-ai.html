The Hardest Part of Building a Voice AI Isn't the AI — It's the Pause

Jahanzeb Kayani 8 min read

I'm a self-taught builder, and I spent a week teaching an AI to talk like a human on the phone — chasing every millisecond of latency out of a real-time inference pipeline. Here's what actually broke — and what I learned.

I'm Jahanzeb. I'm twenty, I study AI and business analytics at the University of South Florida, and I taught myself to code mostly because I got tired of waiting around for permission to build things.

I was born and raised in Pakistan and I'm based in Tampa now. Growing up, the thing that stuck with me was never the hype around technology — it was watching how much of a difference the right tool makes when you actually have access to it, and how unevenly that access gets handed out. A big company can afford a front desk, a full-time receptionist, and software for everything. The small business down the street can't — so every call that slips to voicemail is a job they just lost. That gap is most of why I build. I think AI is the best shot we've had in a long time at closing it.

Cleo came out of that. It's an AI receptionist that answers the phone for home-service businesses — HVAC, plumbing, electrical — books real appointments, and syncs them to whatever tools the business already runs on. No one at the front desk required.

The AI part — understanding the caller, finding an answer, booking the slot — took maybe two days. The other five were spent fighting an enemy I didn't see coming: the pause.

The pause is the whole problem

On a phone call, three seconds of silence is an eternity. You ask a question, there's dead air, and you're already saying "...hello?" before the answer comes. A human fills that gap without thinking. An AI does not — by default it waits until it has the entire response before it says a single word, and generating that response (after transcribing the speech and searching a knowledge base) takes a few seconds. Those few seconds are where voice AI goes to die.

This is really an end-to-end latency problem — speech-to-text, retrieval, LLM inference, and text-to-speech, each adding their own delay, stacked in sequence. I learned this the unglamorous way: by calling my own demo line over and over from my couch, wincing every time at the little robotic gap before Cleo spoke. It sounded like a machine. And I knew that if it sounded like a machine, no real business would ever put it on their main number.

What it does

A customer calls a plumber at 9 PM. Instead of voicemail:

"Apex Home Services, this is Cleo, your virtual receptionist. How can I help you?"

The caller says their water heater is leaking. Cleo asks what's wrong, gets their name (and spells it back to confirm), gets the address, checks the real calendar, offers open slots, books the appointment, and reads it back. By the time they hang up, a job exists in the company's CRM, a confirmation text is sent, and a summary is in the owner's inbox. No human touched it.

The stack: Python/FastAPI, Claude Sonnet for the reasoning layer, Deepgram for speech-to-text, Qdrant for vector retrieval, Twilio for the phone line, Postgres for state — all in Docker on one small server.

The trick that kills the pause

Here's the insight that fixed it.

When you ask Claude something, you can stream the response back via token-level streaming. Most people stream it to a screen. But on a phone you can't speak half a word — text-to-speech needs a full sentence to sound right. So the move is: stream the tokens, and the instant you see the end of the first sentence, ship that sentence off to be spoken — while the rest is still being written.

async for text in stream.text_stream:
    full_text += text
    if not first_yielded:
        for marker in [". ", "? ", "! "]:
            idx = full_text.find(marker)
            if idx != -1:
                first_sentence = full_text[: idx + 1].strip()
                first_yielded = True
                yield "first", first_sentence   # speak this NOW
                break

The caller hears Cleo start answering while Cleo is still finishing the thought — exactly like a real person who starts a sentence before they've fully planned it. It's a distinction between perceived latency and actual latency: the total compute time doesn't change, but the caller experiences almost none of it. The first time I called the line and it answered me back instantly, with no gap, I actually laughed out loud in my apartment. It had crossed the line from "robot" to "receptionist," and it came down to one small decision about when to start talking.

Booking a real appointment, mid-sentence

An AI that only answers questions is a search box with a voice. The hard part is turning it into an agentic system — one that can actually take action, not just retrieve information. Cleo books real appointments using Claude's tool calling — I give it check_availability and book_appointment as callable functions. When Claude decides it needs the calendar, the response comes back with stop_reason == "tool_use":

if final_msg.stop_reason == "tool_use":
    for block in final_msg.content:
        if block.type == "tool_use":
            result = await _execute_tool(block.name, block.input, caller_phone, config)
            tool_results.append({
                "type": "tool_result",
                "tool_use_id": block.id,
                "content": json.dumps(result),
            })
    # feed results back, stream the spoken reply

This is multi-turn tool orchestration — Claude calls a tool, gets the result, and reasons over it in a second pass before responding. The part I'm quietly proud of: the calendar lookup runs between the first and the rest of the spoken response. The caller hears "Let me check what we have available" while the query is actually running. The wait hides behind the sentence — so the slowest part of the whole system is the part you never notice.

The prompt was the product

I thought the code was the hard part. It wasn't. What separates a cool demo from something a business will actually trust on its main line is the system prompt — and prompt engineering turned out to be a real discipline, not an afterthought. Mine grew to over 60 lines of guardrails I learned the hard way, mostly by listening to calls go wrong:

  • Spell names back. "Is that J-O-H-N S-M-I-T-H?" Phone transcription mangles names; confirming saved countless wrong bookings.
  • Never promise an exact time. Cleo gives 2-hour windows, never "we'll be there at 2 PM."
  • Detect emergencies first. Gas smell, flooding, sparks → tell them to call 911 before booking.
  • No markdown, ever. Claude loves bullet points; on a phone they're read aloud as garbage.
  • One question per turn. Stack three and the caller freezes.
  • Stay grounded in real data. Every answer comes from the business's knowledge base via retrieval — never invented prices or policies.
  • Don't fake empathy. No "Oh no!" or "Great question!" — warm but direct, like a real receptionist.

None of that is code. All of it is the difference between a demo and a product.

What I'd do differently

  1. The state is in-memory. Call state lives in dicts keyed by call ID — fine on one box, broken the moment I scale to multiple instances. That belongs in Redis, and I'd reach for it earlier.
  2. The voice is the weak link. I use Twilio's built-in TTS for lowest latency, but it's robotic. The real fix is streaming generated audio back over the WebSocket instead of round-tripping through markup.
  3. Test with bad audio sooner. Everything worked in my tests. Real calls have noise, accents, people talking over Cleo. Half my prompt rules exist because of failures I didn't see until real humans called.

The takeaway

If you take one thing from this: your users feel latency, not intelligence. A slightly less clever answer that arrives instantly beats a brilliant one that lands after an awkward pause. Almost every decision in Cleo — streaming the first sentence, hiding the tool call behind speech, never blocking — exists to kill silence.

The AI turned out to be the easy part. Making it feel like a person was the work. And that's the part I keep coming back to — because the businesses that most need a tool like this are the ones who could never afford the human version of it. Closing that gap is the whole reason I'm building.
